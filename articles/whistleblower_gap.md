# Canada Protects Whistleblowers. The US Pays Them. The Difference Shows.

*Why the gap between two countries' anti-corruption laws costs Canada billions annually*

---

Last year, the US Department of Justice recovered $2.68 billion from fraud cases under the False Claims Act. A large share of those cases started with a tip from someone inside the organization being investigated. Independent estimates put that share above 70%.

Canada has a whistleblower law too. It generates roughly 100 formal disclosures per year. Most of them go nowhere.

The difference is not about how much corruption exists in each country, or the moral character of public servants. It is about incentive design.

---

## What the False Claims Act Actually Does

The False Claims Act dates to 1863, when Congress was trying to stop defense contractors from selling the Union Army sawdust instead of gunpowder. The qui tam provision (Latin for "who as well") lets a private citizen file a fraud lawsuit on the government's behalf.

If the case succeeds, the whistleblower receives 15 to 30 percent of the recovered funds.

On a $10 million fraud, that is $1.5 to $3 million. The FCA has recovered over $75 billion since 1986. Plaintiffs' attorneys take these cases on contingency because the expected returns justify it.

The effect is a distributed fraud detection network. The government does not need to find every corrupt procurement officer. It needs enough insiders to calculate that coming forward is worth it, financially and professionally.

---

## Why the Financial Incentive Is Not About Greed

The standard objection to paying whistleblowers is that it feels wrong. You should report fraud because it is the right thing to do, not because you get paid. This objection is emotionally intuitive and empirically wrong.

Reporting fraud has a real cost. People who blow the whistle on their employers or agencies face retaliation, formal and informal, documented in study after study. They get passed over for promotion. They get frozen out of meetings. Sometimes they get fired and spend years in litigation to get their jobs back. The FCA has anti-retaliation provisions, but legal protection and actual protection are different things.

The financial reward does not corrupt the motivation. It compensates for the risk. A person who reports $10 million in fraud, faces two years of career disruption, and receives $1.5 million has not gotten rich. They have roughly broken even on what the disclosure cost them. Without the incentive, honesty requires accepting a significant personal loss for a diffuse public benefit. Most people do not do that.

This is not a criticism of anyone's character. It is just how incentives work.

---

## What Canada Has Instead

The Public Servants Disclosure Protection Act (PSDPA), passed in 2005, offers federal employees legal protection if they report wrongdoing through official channels. Retaliation against a whistleblower is prohibited. Disclosures go to the Public Sector Integrity Commissioner, who investigates and refers findings to the relevant authority.

There is no financial reward. The protection is real on paper, but the enforcement record is thin. Since 2007, the PSIC has received around 900 disclosures total. Fewer than 10% led to a formal finding of wrongdoing.

The PSDPA also covers only government employees, not contractors or outside parties. A supplier who discovers that a competitor bribed a procurement officer has no mechanism to report it and no incentive to try. In the US, contractors and private citizens can file qui tam actions. In Canada, they cannot.

Procurement fraud that would surface through a tipline in the US persists in Canada because the person who knows about it has nothing to gain and something to lose from coming forward.

---

## The Concrete Cost

Estimating what this gap costs is imprecise, but the components are not mysterious.

The federal government spends roughly CAD$37 billion per year on goods and services across all departments, about CAD$24 billion of which flows through PSPC directly. Conservative OECD estimates suggest 20% of total procurement spending is lost to corruption and structural inefficiency. That puts the annual waste figure somewhere between CAD$4.8 billion and CAD$7.4 billion depending on which scope you use. The portion attributable to fraud that a whistleblower mechanism could surface is a fraction of that, but a meaningful one.

GAO analysis of US procurement fraud consistently finds that cases originating from insider tips recover more money and proceed faster than cases originating from government audits. Audits are scheduled. Insiders know where the bodies are buried.

Canada's Auditor General identifies procurement problems, including the Phoenix pay system, the ArriveCAN contract, and professional services sole-sourcing rates running at twice the OECD average. But those findings come years after the damage is done, through retrospective audit rather than real-time disclosure. A functional qui tam mechanism would not catch everything. It would catch things earlier.

---

## The Case Against Paying Whistleblowers

The counterarguments are worth taking seriously, because they are not all wrong.

The most substantive one is about false claims. Financial incentives attract opportunists. A system that pays for tips will eventually attract people who fabricate or exaggerate allegations hoping for a payout. The FCA has dealt with this: courts have the authority to reduce a relator's share or dismiss a case if the claim is frivolous, and defendants can recover legal fees in some circumstances. But litigation is expensive regardless of outcome, and being the subject of a false qui tam action is genuinely damaging even if the case is eventually dismissed. Critics argue the FCA produces too many weak cases that waste court resources and impose costs on innocent organizations.

A related concern is the effect on workplace culture. If employees know that any allegation might trigger a qui tam action, organizations may become more defensive and less transparent internally. People stop documenting things. Conversations move off the record. The chilling effect on legitimate internal communication may partially offset the benefits of increased external disclosure.

There is also a fairness argument specific to Canada. Public servants in Canada have a different relationship with their employer than contractors in the private sector. They have collective agreements, strong job protections, and independent grievance mechanisms that do not exist in comparable form in the US. Some argue that the US model compensates for a weaker baseline of employment protection, and that Canada's stronger civil service protections partially substitute for a financial reward.

These arguments do not close the gap between 100 Canadian disclosures per year and thousands of US qui tam filings. But they do suggest that a direct transplant of the FCA model would carry real costs alongside the benefits.

---

## The Political Obstacle

Amending the PSDPA to include financial rewards faces a specific cultural resistance in Canada. The objection is rarely stated directly, but it amounts to this: we should not pay people for doing the right thing. Public servants have a duty to report wrongdoing. A reward cheapens the act.

This argument has more purchase in Canada than it does in the US, where the FCA's qui tam provision has a 160-year track record and broad political support. Canadian political culture is more skeptical of financial incentives in public service contexts.

The counterargument is practical. The current system produces 100 disclosures per year and recovers a fraction of what the US system does per capita. Whatever moral satisfaction the non-payment approach provides, it is not recovering the billions lost annually to procurement fraud.

A middle path could thread the needle. Smaller reward percentages, capped amounts, and restrictions to contractor and supplier disclosures rather than public servants would be a narrower change than adopting the full US model. The PSDPA already distinguishes between public servants and outside parties in various ways. Extending qui tam standing to contractors and private citizens, without touching the public servant provisions, would address the most obvious gap while sidestepping the cultural objection almost entirely.

---

## The Takeaway

The gap between the US FCA and Canada's PSDPA is not ideological. Both countries want less procurement fraud. The difference is that one system is designed around how people actually behave under risk, and the other is designed around how policymakers wish they would. The counterarguments about false claims and workplace culture are real, and a Canadian version of the FCA should be designed to account for them. But the current system is not working, and the status quo has costs too. Closing the gap does not require copying the US approach exactly. It requires accepting that financial risk needs financial compensation and building something that reflects that, carefully enough that the cure does not become its own problem.
